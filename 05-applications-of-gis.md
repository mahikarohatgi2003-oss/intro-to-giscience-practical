# Chapter 5 — Applications of GIS

## 5.1 Land Use Mapping

"Land use" and "land cover" are often used interchangeably, but each has a distinct meaning:

- **Land cover** refers to the physical surface material on the ground — vegetation, urban infrastructure, water, bare soil, etc. Identifying land cover establishes the baseline for thematic mapping and change-detection analysis.
- **Land use** refers to the *purpose* the land serves — recreation, wildlife habitat, agriculture, etc.

Together, **Land Use / Land Cover (LULC)** refers to the categorization/classification of human activities and natural elements on the landscape within a given time frame, based on established scientific and statistical analysis methods.

Land cover has been changing across the earth's surface since time immemorial and will continue to — at scales from local to global, and over time frames from days to millennia (Giri, 2012).

## 5.2 Urban Sprawl Analysis

Urban sprawl (also "suburban sprawl" or "urban encroachment") is the unrestricted growth of housing, commercial development, and roads across large expanses of land, with little regard for urban planning.

## 5.3 Forest Monitoring

Crop growth and yield depend on genetic potential of the cultivar, soil, weather, cultivation practices (sowing date, irrigation, fertilizer), and biotic stresses. Historically, year-to-year yield variability has mostly been modeled using weather as a predictor, via empirical or crop-simulation approaches.

With the continuous availability of multi-spectral (visible, near-infrared) sensors on polar-orbiting earth observation satellites (Landsat, SPOT, IRS, etc.), remote sensing (RS) data has become a key tool for yield modeling — offering timely, accurate, synoptic, and objective estimation of crop growing conditions across a range of spatial scales. RS data has advantages over meteorological observation alone: dense coverage, direct crop viewing, and the ability to capture non-meteorological effects.

Integrating **crop simulation models + RS data + GIS** provides a strong solution for monitoring and modeling crops across spatial scales (Dadhwal, 2003).

Forest monitoring systems similarly include Measurement, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) functions, producing reliable data — including forest-carbon estimates — that are critical in the fight against climate change driven by deforestation and forest degradation. Effective monitoring underpins Sustainable Forest Management (SFM): operational monitoring ensures current prescriptions/guidelines are followed and highlights where corrective action is needed.
