# Chapter 6 — Conclusion

Global change research poses significant challenges to the scientific community. Natural scientists (physical and biological) have grappled with data-requirement challenges for decades and have identified satellite remote sensors as major sources of consistent, continuous data for atmospheric, ocean, and land studies across spatial and temporal scales.

Satellite sensor data has proven valuable to atmospheric and ocean sciences. While social scientists may have limited direct involvement in the physical/chemical/biological processes studied by these communities, human-dimensions interests intersect strongly with the *causes* of atmospheric/ocean perturbations and their resulting health and socioeconomic effects.

**Land use vs. land cover**, revisited:
- **Land use** is a human-centric term — the activity practiced on a piece of land.
- **Land cover** is the physical material covering the land surface, regardless of whether humans are using it. Changes in LULC aren't always human-driven; natural forces can also reshape land cover.

GIS's importance lies in letting us view a location holistically, surface patterns and trends otherwise invisible, and make decisions based on the most comprehensive data available — making it an essential tool across real estate, government, and engineering. GIS improves organizational integration by bringing hardware, software, and data together for capturing, managing, analysing, and displaying all forms of geographically referenced information — enabling users to view, question, interpret, and visualize data through maps, globes, reports, and charts.

**This practical file** applied core GIS functions to the state of West Bengal, India (chosen arbitrarily as a study area):
1. The base map of West Bengal was downloaded from internet sources.
2. The map was registered (georeferenced) in QGIS.
3. West Bengal's administrative boundary and districts were digitized in QGIS.
4. Census of India data was joined to the digitized layer.
5. Population, literacy, and household-count analyses were performed and visualized through diagrammatic representations and thematic maps (choropleth, pie, and histogram).
